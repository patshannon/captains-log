# Captain's Log — 2026-03-04
Started the day with something a bit experimental — initializing a LinkedIn bot project. Eleven files in the first commit suggests this isn't just a script thrown together; there's likely some scaffolding, config, and probably an authentication layer involved. I've been wanting to automate some LinkedIn interactions for a while, so this is early days but feels like a solid foundation. Curious to see where it goes.

The bulk of the day, though, was spent hunting down and fixing a cluster of related bugs across two private projects, both revolving around job offer publishing logic.

The most subtle of the three was a date reversal bug in French locale handling. Date formatting is one of those things that looks trivial until it silently corrupts data — DD/MM/YYYY versus MM/DD/YYYY is a classic trap, and the French locale makes it easy to get backwards if you're not careful about which formatter is doing the work. Three files touched, which tells me this wasn't just a one-line parse fix — it probably propagated through a display layer or a serialization step as well.

The second bug was more semantic: when a job offer gets re-approved after modification, the system was overwriting the original `publishedAt` timestamp instead of preserving it. That's the kind of regression that quietly breaks reporting and audit trails. The fix was a single file, which is a good sign — it was a scoping issue rather than an architectural one.

The third piece was additive: making sure `publishedAt` gets included when job offer updates are written through the `publishDueScheduledJobOffers` function. This feels like it was probably the root cause discovery that prompted the re-approval fix — once you start auditing where that field is set, you find all the places it isn't.

These three changes together tell a coherent story: `publishedAt` wasn't being treated as a first-class field throughout the lifecycle of a job offer, and today was the day that got corrected. Small surface area, but high correctness value.
